Atlantic Ocean Circulation Weakening Faster Than Models Predicted

New measurements from an array of deep-sea sensors spanning the Atlantic basin reveal that the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation has weakened by approximately 15 percent since 2004. The decline is roughly twice the rate projected by leading climate models for this period.

The AMOC acts as a giant conveyor belt, carrying warm surface water northward from the tropics and returning cold, dense water southward at depth. Its weakening has immediate consequences for weather patterns across Europe, sea level along the US East Coast, and monsoon systems in Africa and South Asia.

Scientists attribute the acceleration to increased freshwater input from Greenland ice sheet melt, which reduces the salinity and density of surface water in the North Atlantic. This prevents the water from sinking, effectively slowing the entire circulation system. The findings have prompted calls for expanded monitoring infrastructure and updated climate projections.
