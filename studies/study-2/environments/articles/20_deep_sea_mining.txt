International Seabed Authority Delays Deep-Sea Mining Decision Amid Scientific Concerns

The International Seabed Authority has postponed a decision on issuing the first commercial deep-sea mining licenses following a petition signed by 800 marine scientists warning that current environmental knowledge is insufficient to predict the ecological consequences of seabed extraction.

The Clarion-Clipperton Zone in the central Pacific, where most mining claims are concentrated, contains polymetallic nodules rich in manganese, nickel, cobalt, and copper. These nodules take millions of years to form and support unique ecosystems including newly discovered species of sponges, worms, and xenophyophores that depend on the hard substrate for attachment.

Mining companies argue that seabed minerals are essential for the energy transition, as electric vehicle batteries and renewable energy infrastructure require large quantities of the metals found in nodules. Environmental groups counter that improved recycling, material substitution, and reduced consumption could meet demand without destroying deep-sea habitats that remain poorly understood. The ISA has scheduled a new review for next year, commissioning additional environmental impact assessments.
