Heat-Resistant Coral Transplants Show 85 Percent Survival After Marine Heatwave

A large-scale coral restoration project in the Great Barrier Reef has reported that heat-resistant coral fragments survived a severe marine heatwave with 85 percent survival rates, compared to just 30 percent for native corals in adjacent areas. The project transplanted 50,000 fragments of selectively bred Acropora species across 12 reef sites.

The heat-resistant corals were developed through assisted evolution at the Australian Institute of Marine Science, where parent colonies were subjected to progressively warmer conditions over four generations. The resulting offspring express elevated levels of heat shock proteins and harbor thermotolerant strains of symbiotic algae.

While the results are encouraging, marine biologists emphasize that coral restoration cannot substitute for emissions reduction. Even heat-adapted corals face limits, and the pace of ocean warming may eventually exceed the capacity for assisted evolution. The project's lead scientist described the work as buying time for reefs while global climate policy catches up.
